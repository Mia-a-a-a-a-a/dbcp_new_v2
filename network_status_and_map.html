Mapping the seas in real time: the global buoy network at work

The global buoy network

The DBCP network is just one of the observing networks which make up the Global Ocean Observing System. The maintenance and expansion of the DBCP network is done within the context of the other networks and requirements for the integrated system. DBCP monthly map products are available to visualise the buoy network as images (png or pdf format). The interactive Viewers are available through OceanOps viewer.

Maintaining and expanding the network

Whilst the DBCP network of over 1250 drifting buoys and 400 moored buoys was completed in 2005, there are still ongoing challenges in sustaining and improving the network. These are detailed in the DBCP implementation strategy.

One of the ongoing challenges are the increasing needs of barometer upgrade. The measurement of air pressure is important for the weather forecasting. The DBCP aims to have air pressure measurements, using a Barometer Port, on as many buoys as possible focusing on high latitudes and remote ocean areas. An upgrade scheme was introduced to encourage the addition of barometers on drifters.

The buoy network is, in reality, not evenly dispersed across the whole ocean, due to difficulties in deploying buoys in very remote areas (especially the southern ocean) and because the buoys, following currents, drift out of some areas quickly or converge in other areas. This is a major challenge for the DBCP.

The DBCP has an ongoing program of developing and testing new sensors and uses for drifting buoys. More detail is available on the Data Uses page.
